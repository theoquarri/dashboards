Key Operational Findings & Recommended Actions

  1. The procurement decision isn't "which supplier is cheapest" — it's "which supplier is cheapest given my current inventory, demand velocity, and lead time window." With 5 DCs and a mix of domestic (1–3 wk), European (8–12 wk), and Asian (12–16 wk) suppliers, the optimal buy depends on weeks of cover at current throughput. Below 4 weeks of cover, only domestic suppliers can fill in time — regardless of unit cost. Above 8 weeks, international landed cost wins. Connecting these signals into a single reorder decision view is the highest-value operations capability.
  2. Five distribution centres create national reach — but demand velocity differs by region, meaning each DC needs its own reorder logic. New York and LA turn inventory faster than Toronto and Atlanta. A unified view of inventory level, weekly demand, and weeks-of-cover by DC by SKU would shift procurement from calendar-based ordering to demand-triggered replenishment — reducing both stockouts and excess inventory.
  3. Product mix alignment with downstream demand is the core operations challenge. Omega carries mouldings, gallery frames, matboard, glazing, foam board, hardware, machines, and supplies — each with different margin profiles, storage requirements, and demand patterns. Aligning the supply chain (what to order, when, where to stock it) with actual sell-through by customer segment and region is where the most operational value sits.
  4. Customer profitability varies widely — but the analysis to quantify it is currently manual and slow. Framers, designers, hospitality, and retail buyers each have different order patterns, discount structures, and service costs. Deep segmentation by customer, category, SKU, and region would reveal which accounts genuinely drive margin vs which consume disproportionate operational resource.
  5. Excel-based workflows are the backbone of the operation — the opportunity is to automate them, not replace them. Pricing updates, discount reconciliation, production costing, inventory reporting, and financial analysis currently live in spreadsheets. The goal isn't to abandon Excel but to automate the data assembly and reconciliation that feeds it — freeing the team to focus on analysis and decisions rather than data wrangling.
